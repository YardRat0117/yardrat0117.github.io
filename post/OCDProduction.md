## OCD Production: Why and how did I construct this site? 

As my title may suggest, I regret to tell you that my production of this site is nothing more than what an OCD patient may behave, as my incessant need for control and precision has led me to meticulously construct every corner of this blog. This space, more than a mere collection of posts, feels like an extension of my mind—where every detail, every piece of information, is carefully curated, perhaps to the point of obsession.

On one hand, my motivation for creating this blog is clear: it is a means of self-expression and exploration. It allows me to organize and articulate my thoughts, to reflect on my ongoing intellectual journey. Through this platform, I can document and share the ideas that occupy my mind—ideas that range from the abstract realms of philosophy to the more concrete issues surrounding technology and academia. The blog becomes a tool not just for sharing, but for deeper reflection, enabling me to observe the evolution of my own thinking over time.

However, on the other hand, I cannot escape the weight of a certain expectation I place on myself. Unlike what is often celebrated in academic circles as "incremental work"-small, manageable contributions that fit neatly within the current discourse-I find myself rejecting the notion of producing something merely for the sake of output. I want my posts to have real value. I want to avoid the temptation to churn out content that feels hollow or superficial, the kind of content that might pass as a "summary" or "outline" of a paper I’ve read, simply rehashed in my own words. To me, such work, though it might be seen as incremental, lacks the depth and originality that I strive for.

But perhaps even more striking than this abstract rejection of shallow content are some of my more specific, almost obsessive tendencies when it comes to creating this blog. One such behavior is my insistence on writing everything in English, despite being a non-native speaker. While I understand the value of making content accessible to a wider audience, I refuse to resort to writing in my native language—something that would no doubt be easier and more comfortable for me. It’s as if I view the use of English as a form of intellectual rigor, a way to elevate the quality of my writing by imposing this extra layer of complexity. However, this decision often leads to frustration, as it requires me to spend far more time on each post, wrestling with the nuances of a language that doesn’t come naturally to me. It’s a form of self-imposed pressure, and yet I can’t seem to let go of it, believing that writing in English lends a certain universality and seriousness to the work that I can’t achieve otherwise.

Another manifestation of my obsessive tendencies is my refusal to produce any content that feels like a "course-oriented technical post." In other words, I avoid writing posts that merely recap what I've learned in class or summarize the technical aspects of a subject in a straightforward, educational manner. I can't shake the feeling that such content is too mechanical, too detached from the kind of deeper intellectual exploration I’m hoping to foster on this blog. I don’t want my posts to feel like educational assignments, where the purpose is simply to explain or inform. Instead, I aim for something more—something that speaks to my personal journey of understanding, that reflects a deeper engagement with the material. This leads me to exclude many potential topics, particularly those that might fall into the realm of technical tutorials or course summaries, even though they would likely be well-received by a broader audience. I’d rather hold back than produce something that feels "too easy" or "too safe."

In reflecting on these behaviors, I realize that they are, at their core, manifestations of a deeper desire for control. By insisting on writing in English and avoiding course-oriented content, I am trying to impose a sense of intellectual rigor and originality on my work, even if it means making the process more difficult for myself. It’s as though I am trying to create a space where everything has a certain elevated standard, a place where every word carries weight, and every idea is presented with utmost care. But the truth is, these self-imposed standards are often a source of frustration. They lead to delays, anxiety, and a kind of perfectionism that, at times, paralyzes me into inaction.

I often find myself disturbed by the proliferation of "low-quality" content online. It's not just the rise of shallow articles or clickbait; it’s the proliferation of half-formed ideas, poorly researched claims, and the constant cycle of regurgitated thoughts that rarely go beyond surface-level understanding. I believe that in contributing to this noise, we risk not just misleading others but also failing to do justice to the complexity of the topics we address. Even without the expectation of financial gain, creating content that lacks substance feels like a disservice. I have always felt that, as someone engaging in intellectual work, the responsibility lies in producing content that both challenges and enlightens, even if it means fewer posts or more time spent refining each idea.

Yet, there's a counterargument that often enters my mind, one that challenges the rigidity of my standards. "Why not accept that we are limited by our abilities?" some might say. "Why feel ashamed of working within the constraints of what we can do?" It is a thought that suggests a certain humility, a reminder that not every piece of work needs to be revolutionary. Sometimes, simply producing something, no matter how modest, is enough. This line of thinking would tell me that, perhaps, it is acceptable to create within a scope that is limited by my current abilities or circumstances—whether that means writing simpler posts or tackling more familiar ideas.

It is here that the tension between quality and quantity rears its head. On one hand, I am driven to produce work that is meaningful, thoughtful, and well-researched, but on the other, there is an undeniable reality: creation is not always easy, and sometimes the best you can do is what lies within your reach at that moment. Should I lower my expectations and allow myself to engage in lighter, more frequent posts? Should I focus on maintaining a steady flow of content, even if it sacrifices depth? Or should I remain steadfast in my pursuit of high standards, even if it means posting less frequently or requiring more time to produce each piece?

The struggle, I’ve come to realize, is not merely about output—it is about intent. It’s about finding a balance between the desire to contribute meaningful work and the recognition that perfection is an ever-elusive goal. It is the awareness that, in some moments, I may not have the time or energy to craft a masterpiece, but that doesn’t mean what I create is without worth. After all, it is not about never producing less-than-ideal work, but about doing so with intention, with the understanding that each post contributes to my broader journey of self-expression and intellectual growth.

Ultimately, this blog is an ongoing process. It represents a place where I can explore, experiment, and learn. Perhaps I’ll never completely resolve the tension between quality and quantity, but that is okay. The very act of writing and sharing is itself a form of progress. I’ve learned that it’s not just about getting the details perfect, nor is it about publishing something for the sake of having something out there. It’s about creating a space that reflects who I am, where I am, and where I hope to go. And sometimes, that means embracing imperfection, letting go of the need for control, and allowing my thoughts to flow—however they may come.

So, while my tendency to obsess over the minutiae of this site may seem excessive, I believe it speaks to my desire for authenticity. It’s not about proving anything to others; it’s about meeting my own standards of thought and expression. In the end, this blog is not just about what I produce, but about how it allows me to navigate the complexities of my own mind and contribute to the ongoing conversation, even if my voice is just one of many in a sea of countless others.
